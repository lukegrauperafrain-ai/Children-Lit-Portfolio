Literature Discussions

Towers Falling
Jewell Parker Rhodes
January 22
Discussion Leader: Luke Frain
Discussion Questions: Q: Throughout the novel, circles of community are an important motif. They are even the design for section breaks. What are three circles of community that Deja belongs to, and how do they influence her? A: Her classroom, her friend group (Ben and Sabeen), and her family. Her classroom encourages Deja to ask questions. Within her friend group, Deja is challenged to have hard, emotionally-charged conversations — they give her support to talk about things she doesn't know how to. In her family, Deja learns lessons of sacrifice, responsibility, and defense mechanisms. Q: Pop often says that Deja is "too young" to know what happened to the towers. Based on Deja's revelation at the end of the novel, how old do you think is old enough to teach children about 9/11 and other catastrophic events? A: Given that Deja was already familiar with the idea of terrorism, I don't think she was too young to know about 9/11. I think her living in New York and having her family personally experience the horrors of that day means that she had a right to know. Generally 3rd–4th grade is an appropriate time to talk about these events with children, but it is important to be very deliberate and cautious about how one does it. Q: How do easily accessible views of 9/11 on the internet influence children of this age? Is it okay for children to discover the horrors of 9/11 on their own? A: I grew up seeing memes relating to 9/11 on the internet, so I think it's pretty much unavoidable. However, it wouldn't be difficult for a child to intentionally or unintentionally stumble across upsetting footage online, even with restrictions. Q: On page 188, the people on the subway pass around the baklava Sabeen makes. What is the significance of this scene? A: This scene was significant because it was a chance for Ben and Deja to witness the concepts of a diverse community that they had been discussing in their classroom — in a real setting with people of all ages. Q: On page 210, Pops talks about how "working in the towers means you made it." How does this influence the tragedy of 9/11 and our perception of what it means to "make it"? Q: On page 213, Pop recalls the story of Mrs. Able spiraling down the stairwell during the chaos of the towers falling. Why do you think Jewell Parker Rhodes included this scene? How do personal stories influence our understanding of historical events we never witnessed? A: We discussed at what age it is appropriate to learn about these large-scale disasters, whether this should be taught in schools or at home, and how those conversations may differ.
Wishtree
Katherine Applegate
February 12
Discussion Leader: Avery Gould
Discussion Questions: Q: Red is a tree, yet the narration feels human and emotionally perceptive. Why do you think Applegate chose a tree as the narrator? What does Red see or understand that human characters do not? A: Red is an old, wise tree that has witnessed generations of humanity. Having the perspective of a tree includes tidbits on nature — some trees are on the gender binary, some aren't — and emphasizes tradition and things that have existed a long time. Red offers an unbiased perspective and represents a community of animals within the tree as well as the wider human community around it. Feeling pain may be difficult for children to conceptualize. Q: Red ultimately chooses to speak at the end of the novel. What does silence represent, and at what point does silence become harmful? A: This moment fosters agency in kids — there is a lot of lead-up making it a great discussion topic about speaking up (#SEL). Speaking up represents a social justice moment: your voice is significant and can make a big impact. Because Red hadn't spoken up until the end, that moment carries a lot of meaning. Q: The wish tree tradition both brings the community together and becomes the setting for division. How does it symbolize both belonging and exclusion? A: Belonging cannot exist without exclusion. Even as unbiased as Red is, they were used as a tool to hurt Samar. Writing "LEAVE" on Red signifies permanence — "this was a part of our community, why are YOU trying to join it?" The character who carved it is left anonymous because it's more about the belief than the individual. Q: The conflict centers on fear directed toward Samar and her family. How does the novel portray the way fear spreads within a community? Q: Red reflects on cycles of growth, death, and renewal. Do you think the story is ultimately hopeful? Q: Applegate uses spare, simple language. How does the simplicity of the writing influence the emotional weight of the story? A: The simplicity mirrors what an oak tree does for the community.
The Crossover
Kwame Alexander
February 19
Discussion Leader: Sophia Pechin
Discussion Questions: Q: The author uses frequent "basketball rules" throughout the novel. What do you think the purpose of these rules is? A: Underlying themes like brotherhood, conflict, and family relations. Teams are like families. Basketball is used to process life because life doesn't have rules but basketball does. Q: Themes of social injustice are woven throughout. Why might Alexander choose basketball as the framework for exploring these injustices? A: More realistic and approachable, easy to grasp and make sense of. Is this a limiting map? Children of color are more than basketball players — more of a mirror than a map. Other factors are more important than basketball. Q: Josh and JB's dad struggles with health complications and fears doctors. What effect does this have on Josh and JB and how does it connect to male vulnerability? A: Kids are impressionable to their parents' ideals. There is a stereotype that men don't need to ask for help — reinforcing ideals, especially with the dad's death at the end, helps make sure it's okay to ask for help. The dad being Black and his father also dying from this disease adds another layer. Lack of transparency leaves Josh fragile. Q: Kwame Alexander displays words representing actions (see pg. 149, pg. 221). What type of poem does this remind you of? What does it add to the story? A: Makes it really fun for the reader. Adds to action — fast paced and exciting. Resemblance to hip-hop and the beat of basketball. Works well because of balance. Q: After Josh and JB lose their dad, Josh states he was "unprepared for death." What does the word "unprepared" signal about how loss affects young people? A: Shows how they took him for granted. They never thought their parents would pass away. Their dad was their biggest role model. Q: At the end of the novel, JB slides their dad's championship ring onto Josh's finger. What does this represent? A: Passing of the guard — it's time to grow up now. Take what he taught you and live in his honor. (Why just Josh?) Q: As the story progresses, Josh and JB's relationship grows and changes. What does the story illustrate about sibling love and brotherhood? Q: Josh and JB constantly feel the expectation to live up to their dad's legacy. Does the pressure motivate them to succeed or prevent them from developing their own identities? Where the Mountain Meets the Moon
Grace Lin
February 26
Discussion Leader: Luke Frain
Discussion Notes: Change in Ma's Stories + Turning Point: - Character development shows growth and change from within — parallel and reflection of story, in steps and progression. - Came back to make a connection to Ma and Ba's encounter with the goldfish man while out looking for Minli, and they decide to go back home. Name one thing Minli took with her on her journey: - A singular bronze coin — trying to give to orphan boy with buffalo, then almost not being able to get rid of it. - Bamboo + rice bowl + water to make a compass to help on her journey. Why end with the Goldfish Man? - Wrapping everything up with what started it all. Goldfish symbolism: fate, the choices she made along the way. He wasn't along the journey — she had the answer all along. Paper of Happiness — thankfulness: - We see thankfulness (or the lack of it) in every single character. - Why choose "thankfulness" over "gratitude"? It doesn't matter what one's wealth status is — it's free to be thankful. - Why couldn't they read it at the beginning of the book? It's hard to be thankful unless you are intentional about it (similar to hearing the fish when you "believe"). What story stood out the most? - Wu Kang's lesson being the Village of Moon Rain's blessing. - Tale of the Dragon — #friendship. - Village/family who were always happy; Tiger Magistrate very angry. Why did this book win the Newbery Medal? - Symbolism. Writes about children in a realistic, not overly naive or misconstrued way.
Becoming Naomi León
Pam Muñoz Ryan
March 5
Discussion Leader: Jennifer Andrews
Discussion Questions: Q: Which character left the strongest emotional or personal impact on you? A: Most significant characters: Naomi, Gram, Owen, Skyla, Clive, Blanca, Bernardo & Fabiola, Father (Santiago). - Gram: Taking action, knows what's right and that she needs to stand up for her kids. "I'd give up everything, and now I am" — disregarding her Wheel of Fortune streak because she cares most about her kids. - Naomi: Looking out for Owen, bravery, resilience through trauma with Skyla. Transition with finding her voice — more worried about speaking up and saying the wrong things → court scene where she becomes her own person. Q: Why do you think Pam Muñoz Ryan titled the book Becoming Naomi León rather than simply Naomi León? A: The entire book is the becoming. It's incremental — experiences that build who you are. Q: How did you interpret Naomi's silence while reading? A: History: selective mutism. Naomi caught between two worlds — wants to please Skyla and have a mother, but Gram and Owen are her family that she knows and that have always been supportive. Bilingual children may begin to speak later. Mostly trauma-induced. Q: What cultural details stood out to you most? A: Catholic influences — Soledad, saints and churches. Carving — very cool to read about. Trailer Park: you can be content even if you don't have many physical things. She's happier without her mother in a trailer park. Q: How effectively does the book handle substance abuse, being raised by a guardian, and characters with birth defects/deformities? - Substance abuse: When can it be appropriately introduced to children? Elementary–middle school. Hard/confusing read without an adult to scaffold. The back of the book doesn't even mention alcohol — parent backlash possible. - Characters with birth defects/deformities: Connection to Wonder. What's the symbolism/deeper meaning with the tape? Coping mechanism (form of stimming?). Fetal Alcohol Syndrome? Because of the mother? Maybe she dislikes her son because of how he appears — and because she realizes it's a result of her actions. Q: What assumptions does the author seem to make about her readers? (Did not get to answer.)
A Place at the Table
Sharon Heal
March 19
Discussion Leader: Mia Becher
Discussion Questions: Q: What does the title mean to you in the context of the story? A: Accepting and including everyone. Thanksgiving. Q: How was food symbolic in the story? A: Food is symbolic because it's both a way characters connect to their own culture and to each other's culture — a gateway into the new culture (window and mirror). The British mom being unable to cook was emphasized comically. Q: Why do you think the author made Maddie and Stephanie (the bad guys) win the cook-off? A: It was realistic — Stephanie has her own cooking business. The author may have wanted things to be explicit and obvious. But it's not about the food — it's about the journey!! Q: How was racism and bullying addressed in the story (overtly, subtly, etc.)? A: Overtly — when she yelled "these Arabs need to leave." Q: In what context should this book be read (full class, independently, grade)? A: 6th grade, maybe 5th — summer reading. Could be used to introduce symbolism and motifs because it is more straightforward. Q: How did the dual narrative format contribute to your reading experience? How might it be received by children? A: The dual narrative perspective was found to be redundant by some. Another student said it offered multiple perspectives. This could be harder for children to understand. Elizabeth had a lot of responsibilities and acknowledged it. Topics around citizenship were appreciated — not biased in any way, but shows people's perspectives.
Merci Suárez Changes Gears

Discussion Questions: Q: How does Merci's Cuban heritage influence her worldview? In what ways is her culture both grounding and complicating? Q: Discuss Edna Santos — how does she embody or challenge traditional "mean girl" tropes? A: One student pitied/sympathized with her; said her determination was admirable and her evilness stemmed from insecurity. Another student sympathized when her eyebrows were bare — must be hard for middle school. Q: Merci experiences embarrassment, jealousy, and guilt. Why is it important for middle-grade literature to explore these more uncomfortable themes? A: How Merci grew through them — proud of Lolo. Q: How might this book function differently for child readers versus adult readers? A: Students may find this text more relatable and comprehensible while adult readers may be more accustomed to learning about children from the outside. Q: If you could rewrite one moment in the book, what would it be and why? A: Highlighting the weird social dynamics between boys and girls in middle school was well done. Relationship with the brother was very well done. Q: Why do you think Meg Medina chose to tell the story from Merci's point of view rather than an adult's perspective? Q: What is the effect of having readers learn about Lolo's condition gradually, just as Merci does? Q: Why does Medina place such a strong contrast between Merci's home life and her school environment? Q: Why is the title Merci Suárez Changes Gears such an effective title? In what ways does it work both literally and symbolically?
Ghost Boys
Jewell Parker Rhodes
April 9
Discussion Leader: Jennifer Andrews
Discussion Questions: Q: How well does the writing style capture and present complex, sensitive topics such as racial prejudice and police brutality for readers aged 10–15? A: The Florida Fraternal Order of Police labeled the book "propaganda that pushes an inaccurate and absurd stereotype of police officers." However, it's not a stereotype — it's one police officer in this story. Written as realistic fiction, the events are undeniably tied to real events (Emmett Till, Tamir Rice). For readers 10–15, this is a critical time for impression formation and critical thinking. The writing conveys deep topics in an accessible manner, and the supernatural component maintains interest and appeal. Scaffolding is needed for younger readers. Q: How is this novel different from or similar to the others we have read? A: In terms of social issues, other books mostly look at interpersonal issues, while this is focused on more violent topics with weapons and murder. The toy gun represents naivety. Complexity: the other kids also thought it was real, but the 911 operator was aware of the possibility of the gun being a toy and failed to report that to the officers. Other books look at family structures, but this book is probably the most relevant because these things happen every day. Including Sarah — someone naturally bound to be on her father's side — learning and empathizing and trying to help Jerome. Q: The book features many ghost boys, not just Jerome and Emmett. What are your thoughts regarding the author's choice to write about a world where such ghost boys exist? A: This symbolizes the historical and current reality of the world. The idea that each ghost has one person who sees them (p. 108). Q: Share your thoughts on Jerome, Sarah, and Officer Moore: - Sarah: Wanted to look at the video footage, learning more, trying to talk to her father. Modeling bravery, social justice, critical thinking. Teaches kids that they don't have to believe exactly what their parents believe. - Officer Moore: He knows he did something wrong — gaslighting himself throughout the novel until the end. "The young man I killed?" At the end, he realizes he was in the wrong but remains hesitant to call Jerome a boy.
Refugee
Alan Gratz
April 16
Discussion Leader: Sophia Pechin
Discussion Questions: Q: The author uses the viewpoints of three different children to convey his themes. What effect does this have on the novel? A: Which story stood out most? - Mahamoud: connections between the other timelines. Not something that happened 200 years ago — still possibly relevant today. - Josef: the plot point where the mom had to choose between her children. Q: The book portrays many moments that display the harsh realities of refugees. What moments stood out to you and why? A: They all had a sacrifice — it wasn't just the parents; the kids had to make sacrifices too. A better way to introduce these topics to children. Q: In the context of the world today, how can this book be a mirror, window, and sliding glass door? A: More of a window into the past. Possibly not a mirror for students today. Q: Many chapters stop right after something significant happens. What purpose do the cliffhangers serve? A: Keep the reader engaged and excited. Replicates reality. Q: Mahamoud's family is escaping toward Germany while Josef's family wants to leave Germany. Isabel's family wants to leave Cuba for the United States. How does this form a "circle" across countries? A: Comes in relation to time. Depends on individual identity. Q: How are the three characters' stories similar even though they happened at different locations and time periods? A: A lot of the time parents have to make quick, rash decisions and then fall apart — kids must take over. Q: The ending reveals a hidden truth connecting the three stories. What does this suggest about generational responsibility? A: Sometimes it's necessary to become a refugee for a better life — we must move toward making the process easier and have empathy for others.

Learning Experience A — Watercress & Africa Dream
Picture Book Essays
Watercress / Africa Dream
Watercress Like Jason Chin writes in his note, "I am impressed by how Andrea was able to fold so many layers of memory, culture, and emotion into such a short text." There really aren't many words in the manuscript, yet the story feels complete. As a writer, this challenges me to think about where I am using space ineffectively, as well as how I can resolve this. After all, most picture books must be only thirty-two pages or so. One way that I notice Andrea writes succinctly is by allowing the pictures to tell part of the story. She does this sometimes by leaving a whole page blank for an illustration, like the second page that shows the family driving or the thirteenth page that shows them loading the watercress back into the car. What is more impressive to me, though, are the pages that have a few words telling some of the story and a picture telling the rest. For instance, on the eighteenth page, Andrea doesn't explicitly write that the girl wore hand-me-down clothes growing up or that her parents collected trash-heap furniture. She only writes that these things are free and, by association, bad. However, the pictures provide context, so we know why she feels this way. Another example of a picture telling part of the story is on the twenty-second page. Andrea doesn't write that the girl's uncle died, but she doesn't have to. By writing "it was not enough" alongside a picture of his figure missing from the table, we can infer that the famine killed him. To me, these two scenes are the most resonant of the book. Of course, this style of writing wouldn't be effective without a skilled illustrator, a qualification that is certainly true of Jason Chin. He is also a skilled speaker, and I enjoyed listening to his Caldecott acceptance speech. It was very relatable, even when he talks about a Chinese culture that I know so little about, and it was fun listening to him remember painting castles and dragons and all that was his creative childhood. Perhaps my favorite line from the speech is, "To breathe life into your art, put your whole self into it, and become one with what you are painting." Coming from many other people, I would probably laugh at this saying, but I can really feel the artistic commitment in Jason's paintings. From the landscapes to facial expressions, every brush stroke is accounted for with the same soft quality. There is unity in the details of every picture, which compliments the simplicity of the text. It's ridiculous to think that Jason was reluctant to collaborate on this project at first. I'm glad that Andrea encouraged him to share his memory and tell his story. As she writes in her note, "They are essential." Finally, it was fascinating to hear Jason talk about his struggles to understand the theme of hope in Watercress. He conveyed it so well in the pictures, and it's a reminder that one doesn't have to always possess all of the answers to craft a compelling piece of art. He let Andrea's text guide him, and in that process, he revealed a theme he didn't need to know. --- Africa Dream This book seems to reflect our thoughts on representation in literature earlier this semester. I remember us discussing how we don't necessarily need to tell stories with people of color as superheroes; we simply need to paint them in a positive light in everyday situations, and readers will know that they can do anything a white person can. We just need to plant a seed of fairness, and our imagination can do the rest. On this note, I think that this book's greatest strength is its ability to paint African life in a positive light. When I read this book, I can't help but want to visit, to acquaint myself with the people that call this place home. This positivity comes partly from the manuscript — the girl dreams of going to Africa, a place where she is welcomed, a place that she understands. Here she can sing and dance freely until she grows tired, at which point she knows she can lean back into the comfort of her mama's arms. This positivity also comes from the illustrations. The pictures are playful, filled with smiles and warm embraces. They also have a nostalgic quality that comes from the black and white charcoal. Regardless, the words and pictures paint Africa as a place that is worthy, that is desirable. Another strength of this book is placing objects of cultural significance in the spotlight, instead of defaulting to objects of Americans. In the book, the girl doesn't eat apples — she eats mangoes. She doesn't ride in a car — she rides on a donkey. Of course, it is easy for such cultural references to turn to stereotypes, but I think it's more important for the book not to be whitewashed. These people have their own culture that is worthy of being represented. In thinking about why this book deserves the Coretta Scott King Award for illustration specifically — an award that goes to artists who best reflect "the African American experience" — I think that Carole Byard does an excellent job of displaying the struggles and rewards of African American identity, of taking pride in a homeland that is yours but that you might have never been to. For an African, this book might be nostalgic. For an African American, this book might mean a bit more. It is, forgive me, like a window.
Learning Experience C — Elmore Discussion Guide
2nd Grade
Elmore
First Discussion Topic (after 1st reading): What does Elmore teach us about making friends? Vignette #1 (p. 5) — Why does Elmore want to make friends? Because although he is a solitary porcupine, Elmore often feels lonely. Vignette #2 (p. 10) — Why is Elmore rejected at first? Because the other animals think he is too prickly — it's hard to be around him because then you get needled, nettled, and prickled. Vignette #3 (p. 17) — What does his uncle say about Elmore's quill troubles? That Elmore's quills make him who he is, that they are handsome and he should treasure them. Vignette #4 (p. 22–24) — How does Elmore use his uncle's advice to make friends and become less lonely? What does this teach us about the best friendships? Elmore turns his quills into pens which he hands out to the other animals for free. The animals love their pens, sending him thank you notes and finally accepting him. This teaches us that the best friendships come from embracing who we are and sharing that with others. It is easier for others to love us when we love ourselves. --- Second Discussion Topic (after 2nd reading): Why are Elmore's quill pens the perfect gift? Elmore's quill pens are meaningful because they foster connection between the animals. Just like Elmore desires a friend to connect with, the other animals also want to be connected. Elmore's quill pens are also meaningful because they are unique. I would ask this question as a follow-up because it shows how the things we want are often the things others want too. It also shows how something that is common for one person can be a really special gift to someone else. Elmore has hundreds, if not thousands of quills, yet the other animals are delighted to receive them.
Learning Experience D — Banned Book Poem
Poem
Book Banning / Free Expression
The school banned a book today, we're not allowed to read it. I never wanted to read it before, but now I simply need it! The school banned the book because a senator was upset. I'm not sure what upset them, but it's a book I've got to get. I searched the school library on every shelf and nook, I searched high and low but couldn't find the book. Where do banned books go? Is there an "Island of the banned?" Are they shipped off far away? To some forbidden land? The parents are picketing, the politicians are mad. I don't know what is in that book, but it must be pretty bad! The book has caused a stir in town, of that there is no doubt. I've asked, but no one seems to know what the book's about! We talked about the book today while riding on the bus. Finally, we figured out what was causing all the fuss. The book is about a duck, who happens to be gay. Some people fear if you read the book then you will "turn that way!" A waterfowl in a children's book is causing quite a ruckus, No other book has ever caused the stir that little duck is. I didn't know ducks were dangerous, but I've got to take a look! I've got to know what that duck is doing inside that forbidden book! Gay, Gay, Gay, it's a word you cannot say! If you say that word, they will take your book away! So what! Who cares if the duck is married to a drake. Why should anyone care what happens in the privacy of their own lake! The man on the TV called the book a "liberal plot"! The librarian on the TV said that "It is not!" The preacher in the pulpit said "if you read it, you'll be damned!" But I once chewed gum in church, so I think I already am. On the radio they are shouting about the author and his plan! "Protect our kids!" They yell as they boycott and they ban. "They should throw the book at him, lock him up, throw away the key!" If they are going to throw the book, I wish they would throw that book at me! I really want to read it, but I don't know where to look? All the schools and libraries have banned that "awful" book! My parents took me to the bookstore, and there on display Was the banned book about the duck who happened to be gay. My parents read through it, and said that it was fine. Now at last I have the book, and it's mine all mine! I rushed home and read it and the duck has two fathers. What? that's it? That's all? What was all the bother? I liked the book a lot, but still don't understand, Why anyone thought a book about a duck, should be banned. Oh… and… If you are thinking I turned gay, then you are out of luck. Because after I read the book, I turned into a duck.

Reading Record

1.
Thankful
Elaine Vickers
Illus: Samantha Cotterill
2021 · Simon & Schuster
Content (Rating: 2): The content of this book is pretty simple. According to tradition, a girl makes paper chains during the first snowfall of the year and writes things that she is thankful for on each one. She calls these "thankful chains." The book follows her as she thinks of what to write on these chains. This structure leaves the book relatively plotless – it's just a list. However, the list is so sweet and well-crafted that I'm not downgrading this section past a 2. Writing Style (Rating: 1): The writing style is on the simple side, but like the content, it's so well-crafted that I'm giving it a 1. The way the girl categorizes the things she is thankful for seems natural and interesting, and it makes the list come to life. There is also something so endearing to the text's simplicity, like for the poem her parents whisper to her: "Good night, dream sweet things, let them carry you on their wings, and whatever tomorrow brings, I love you." Illustrations (Rating: 3): I want to clarify that I do like the illustrations for this book. In particular, I like how the characters all look like cut-outs against the backdrops. This is something I haven't seen done before, or if I have, it wasn't as memorable. I also like how the cover is just a bunch of thankful chains. Again, it's simple but super colorful, and it really grabbed my attention. All this said, I've seen much more detailed illustrations. Overall (Rating: 2): I like this book! It's so well-crafted, and I think it's a great reminder of all the things we can be thankful for. I also appreciate how it's not in the context of Thanksgiving. After all, we can be grateful all year long!
2.
Door
JiHyeon Lee
Illus: JiHyeon Lee
2017 · Iyagikot
Content (Rating: 2): This wordless picture book follows a boy as he leaves a bitter, black and white world after unlocking a door. Through this door, he discovers a new world, full of color and life. Here, he makes a friend who leads him around this place, which seems to have many doors through which characters like himself enter. Ultimately, these characters go to a wedding and celebrate together before the boy leaves. Writing Style (Rating: 3): It's hard to rate the writing style since there aren't any words! There are only squiggles in speech bubbles next to the characters, and readers have to guess what they are saying based on the illustrations. I do think this is an interesting choice as opposed to leaving no markings whatsoever. Illustrations (Rating: 1): The artistic style isn't the most impressive. All the pictures look like colored pencil sketches. Still, the way the pictures tell the story is extremely impressive, which is why I'm giving it a 1. In particular, the contrasting emotions between the world the boy came from and the world through the door is very interesting. There's also this lovely sense of creativity and adventure that comes across through the pictures. Overall (Rating: 2): This is a fascinating book, and I'm really glad I came across it. Perhaps the thing I most appreciate about the work is its universality. Overall (Rating: 1-): I would give this book a 1 if I had a better sense for the overall message. In particular, I was left wanting an explanation of the two grandmothers. Other than that, I thought the story was nice, and it's a nice representation of Eastern and Western mythologies.

I'll Go and Come Back
Rajani LaRocca
Illus: Sara Palacios
2022 · Candlewick
Content (Rating: 1-): This book is about a girl who flies to visit her relative in India one summer. She doesn't understand Tamil, which her grandmother speaks, and their cultures are different, but they make fond memories together. The next summer, the relative visits the girl. I didn't find anything about the content very original. However, it's a story that needs to be told since so many have relatives living on the other side of the world. Writing Style (Rating: 1-): I thought the writing style was fine. It was very simple, and somewhat like the plot, it wasn't very original. Still, there is a feeling of love that shines through the words on the page, and that has to count for something. After all, originality isn't quite as important as how the story makes you feel. Illustrations (Rating: 1): I like these illustrations. They are done in gouache and acrylic and have a nice storybook feel to them. Parts of them also felt like nice windows into India, which was nice for me as an American. I also like the endpapers. Overall (Rating: 1-): It's hard for me to give this book anything less than a 1 since this story is one that so many people can relate to, and it makes the reader feel something. I just wish it was a bit more original. Perhaps some more details about their specific relationship or some more niche activities that they do together.

The Curious Why
Angela DiTerlizzi
Illus: Lorena Alvarez Gómez
2024 · Hachette
Content (Rating: 1): This book is a poem about how when everything seems dull and there's nothing to do, simply being inquisitive can unlock so many doors. It's about all the exciting things that happen when you start to ask how, what, where, and when. It's about how fun learning can be, which is such an important message for young readers to see. Writing Style (Rating: 1): The book is a poem, and it's very whimsical. Some of the rhymes feel a bit forced, but others work so well. I will say that the attention to detail in these pictures is great though. Overall (Rating: 1): This is such an important book that teaches children how fun it can be to ask questions and learn. The fact that it rhymes is the cherry on top. This is a book that I would read to any classroom, from kindergarten all the way through elementary school. It's never too late for a reminder to ask why.
